How third parties can swing elections

By Jeff Hargarten • Star Tribune

Multiple third-party candidates running in 2020's political contests across the country are resurrecting questions about their actual impact on big elections.

Do third party challengers siphon off votes from major-party candidates?

Minnesota's recent past suggests the answer is yes.

The 2016 U.S. House race in Minnesota's Congressional District 2 – viewed in the light of 2018's rematch – highlights how a minor-party candidate can split votes away from ideologically-similar major party opponents.

Most precincts in 2018 had Democratic percentages either over or even (within two points) the combined 2016 totals of Craig and Overby, while only some totals were less than the previous cycle, mostly in areas that went more Republican overall.

The three-way 2016 contest between Jason Lewis (R), Angie Craig (DFL) and Paula Overby (GP) gained further clarity after 2018's rematch between Craig and Lewis.

A Star Tribune analysis of District Two's precinct-level comparing 2016 to 2018 shows Craig's totals against Lewis matched Craig and Overby's split vote two-thirds of the time.

While turnout was different between the presidential year contests and this last midterm, comparing vote percentages reveals a stark trend of Craig gaining in proportions very similar – and in some cases virtually identical – to Overby's totals in 2016.

About a third of the time, Craig's 2018 vote percentage surpassed the combined non-Republican vote in 2016, signaling areas in the district became more Democratic between elections.

Craig fared worse than the combined 2016 outcomes in only a few precincts where Lewis performed better than previously.
