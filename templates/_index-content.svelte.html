How third parties can swing elections: a 2018 retrospective

By Jeff Hargarten • Star Tribune

Data-driven hindsight shows third-party presence can have a sizeable influence on political races.

In 2016, then-candidate Angie Craig (DFL) suffered a bruising loss to Jason Lewis (R) in Minnesota's Second Congressional District, in part due to the Green Party's Paula Overby sharing space on the ballot.

The impact third-party candidates have on politics races is hotly debated. But comparing 2016's results to 2018's, when Craig beat Lewis in a rematch, shows that Overby's absence from that latter race significantly impacted the results.

A Star Tribune analysis shows precincts in 2018 reported Democratic percentages either over or even (within two points) the combined 2016 totals of Craig and Overby. Only some totals were less than the previous cycle, mostly in areas trending Republican.

Ovarall, Craig's 2018 totals closely matched her and Overby's combined leftwing vote from 2016.

Data source: Minnesota Secretary of State | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

About a third of precincts voted more Republican than in 2016 as political polarization strengthened its hold across the state. Craig fared better than 2016's combined leftwing vote in only a few precincts.

As 2020’s election cycle revs up to full speed, multiple independent challengers are slated to run for seats spanning from local elections to federal races. Whether this coming election showcases similarly contentious multiparty races remains to be seen.
